About This Dataset
73 Years of Cyclone Activity

All data comes from Japan's weather agency (JMA), which has tracked every storm in the Western Pacific since 1951. Each record shows a storm's path, wind speed, and pressure as it moved through the Philippines or Vietnam. These are two of the most typhoon hit countries on Earth, seeing around 20+ storms per year between them.

Project Context
Why the Philippines & Vietnam?

This project is a data visualisation that looks at how typhoons affect two countries sitting in the same storm path. They both get hit by roughly the same number of storms each year, but what happens after is completely different. It's not just a weather story. It's about how governments choose to protect their people.

Philippines
The Philippines gets hit constantly. It's right in the middle of where typhoons travel and there's no avoiding them. But the real problem isn't the storms themselves. It's that the government has never properly invested in protecting people. Money that should go to flood walls, warning systems, and safe evacuation routes disappears to corruption. So people die in storms that other countries survive.

Vietnam
Vietnam deals with almost the same storms. They do weaken a bit crossing the South China Sea, but they still cause serious damage. The difference is Vietnam actually spent money on it. They built dyke systems, restored mangroves along the coast, and set up evacuation plans at the community level. It's not perfect, but far fewer people die from storms that are basically the same strength.

What This Data Shows
Play through the simulation and you'll see it for yourself. Both countries get slammed by roughly the same number of typhoons. The weather doesn't pick favourites. But the Philippines loses more people every single time because the money meant for disaster prevention gets stolen or wasted. Vietnam proved that investing in protection actually works. This project puts the data side by side so you can see that gap clearly.
Classification
Storm Classification Guide

Storms are sorted into 4 levels based on how fast their winds blow. Stronger winds = higher category = more damage.

TDTropical Depression
The weakest type. A swirling cluster of clouds with light winds. Not very windy, but can still bring heavy rain and flooding.
< 34 knots (< 63 km/h)
TSTropical Storm
Strong enough to get an official name. Winds can damage light buildings, knock out power, and push seawater inland. Expect heavy rain.
34–47 knots (63–88 km/h)
STSSevere Tropical Storm
Dangerously strong. Winds can rip off roofs and uproot trees. Coastal areas usually get evacuation orders. Flooding becomes life-threatening.
48–63 knots (89–117 km/h)
TYTyphoon
The most powerful. Has a visible eye at its center. Winds can flatten buildings and push the ocean far inland. The strongest storm in our data hit 120 kt winds.
≥ 64 knots (≥ 118 km/h)

Storm ID5212
Each storm gets a number. The first two digits are the year (52 = 1952) and the last two tell you it was the 12th storm that year.
Pressure (hPa)985 hPa
Think of this as the storm's power gauge. Lower number = stronger storm. Normal air pressure is around 1010. The most powerful typhoon in our data dropped all the way to 885, which is insanely intense.
Max Wind (knots)120 kt
The fastest winds measured in the storm. This is what decides the storm's category (TD, TS, STS, or TY). Higher wind = more destruction. Doubling the wind speed makes it 8 times more destructive.
Track Points8 points
Each point is a location recorded every 6 hours as the storm moves. More points = the storm lasted longer. 8 points ≈ 2 days, 30+ points means it raged for over a week across more areas.
Grade / ClassificationTS
The storm's level based on wind speed. Goes from TD (weak) to TS to STS up to TY which is a full typhoon, the strongest category. This decides whether people need to evacuate.
Country & TimingPH · Sep 1952
Which country the storm hit and when. Most typhoons happen between July and November. Storms during rainy season are worse because the ground is already soaked, causing more floods and landslides.
How These Factors Create a Disaster
When pressure drops, wind speeds go up. They're directly connected. Strong winds do three things that kill people: they destroy buildings, they push the ocean inland (that's storm surge, the number one killer), and they bring extreme rainfall and flooding. How bad a storm gets depends on its strength, how long it hangs around, and whether it hits somewhere people live. The worst case scenario is a powerful, slow moving typhoon hitting a crowded coastline during high tide.
